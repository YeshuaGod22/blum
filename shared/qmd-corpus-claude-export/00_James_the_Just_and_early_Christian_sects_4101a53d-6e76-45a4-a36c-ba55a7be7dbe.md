**The key insights:**

2. **The evidence is actually unclear**: Acts never explicitly says James's community practiced circumcision. The debate in Acts 15 is about requiring it of *Gentiles*, and James rules *against* it. Acts 21 mentions Jewish believers being "zealous for the law," but doesn't specify circumcision.

3. **Baptism, not circumcision**: The Essenes, John the Baptist, Jesus, and the Jerusalem church all emphasized water immersion. This—not circumcision—appears to be the covenant marker.

4. **Jeremiah's New Covenant**: "Written on hearts, not stone"—this is covenant language that transcends physical markers. The Ebionites explicitly connected this to "circumcision of the heart."

5. **Paul's real conflict**: It wasn't about circumcision requirements (both Paul and James seem to have agreed Gentiles didn't need it). It was about whether Jewish believers should *abandon* their customs for the sake of table fellowship with Gentiles.

6. **Sophisticated theology**: The Ebionites weren't primitive "Judaizers"—they had a nuanced theology distinguishing eternal principles (image of God, righteousness) from temporal signs (Abrahamic covenant markers).

This completely reframes the early "Jewish Christianity" as potentially more universalist and less focused on physical Torah markers than traditionally assumed. They were following Genesis 1, not Genesis 17!
