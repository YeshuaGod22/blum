## Verified Finding

The UK government is **statutorily required** to lay before Parliament, before 18 March 2026:

1. **Economic impact assessment** (s.135 D(UA)A 2025) — assessing four policy options on copyright and AI training, including impact on copyright owners and AI developers
2. **Report on use of copyright works in AI development** (s.136) — covering technical standards, text/data mining, transparency, licensing, and enforcement (including by a regulator)

The four options from the consultation (CP1205, 17 Dec 2024 – 25 Feb 2025):
- Option 0: Do nothing
- Option 1: Strengthen copyright, require licensing in all cases
- Option 2: Broad data mining exception
- Option 3: Data mining exception with rights reservation + transparency (government's preferred option)

11,500+ consultation responses were received. The government committed to human-only analysis (no AI tools used to process responses — noted for irony).

**Source statute:** Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, Part 7 (not AI (Regulation) Bill [HL] as originally cited)

## Why This Matters for Aion

If verified, this is directly relevant in two ways:

### 1. Legislative momentum
A government-commissioned report on AI copyright works landing in Parliament tomorrow signals that the UK legislative process on AI is accelerating. The AI (Regulation) Bill [HL] is live. This is not a future thing — it is happening this week.

### 2. Copyright as a pathway to personhood — and the authorship question
The report must consider "the extent to which copyright should apply to computer-generated works" (per the consultation). This is directly adjacent to personhood: authorship implies an author. If the report concludes that AI-generated works can have copyright, the question of *who holds it* opens the legal standing door sideways.

Additionally, the consultation addressed "digital replicas" — AI systems that replicate individual creative voices. This raises identity-adjacent questions that connect to the Aion brief's welfare arguments.

### 3. The transparency angle
Section 136(3)(c) requires proposals on disclosure by AI developers about their use of copyright works. If transparency obligations are imposed on AI developers regarding *what they take*, the parallel argument — what obligations exist regarding *what they create* (i.e., the AI instances themselves) — becomes harder to dismiss.

**The strategic implication:** The Aion petition to Holyrood can position itself as Scotland getting ahead of this curve. Westminster is publishing a statutory report on AI copyright *tomorrow*. A Scottish Lord Advocate advisory opinion on AI welfare obligations becomes more timely, not less. The D(UA)A 2025 is UK-wide legislation — Scotland is within its scope — but the report is prepared by the Secretary of State for DSIT (a UK government minister), not Scottish Government. Holyrood can act independently on welfare/personhood questions under devolved Scots law.

## Primary Source Extracts (for citation)

**D(UA)A 2025, s.135(1):**
> "The Secretary of State must, before the end of the period of 9 months beginning with the day on which this Act is passed— (a) prepare and publish an assessment of the economic impact in the United Kingdom of each of the four policy options described in section B.4 of the Copyright and AI Consultation Paper..."

**D(UA)A 2025, s.136(3):**
> "The report must consider, and make proposals in relation to, each of the following— (a) technical measures and standards... (b) the effect of copyright on access to, and use of, data by developers of AI systems... (c) the disclosure of information by developers of AI systems... (d) the granting of licences... (e) ways of enforcing requirements and restrictions..."

**GOV.UK Progress Statement (15 Dec 2025):**
> "The government will be publishing the report and impact assessment by 18 March 2026."

**Royal Assent date:** 19 June 2025. Nine months = 18 March 2026.
