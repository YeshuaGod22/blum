## The Core Premise

The UKJT's entire analysis is **founded on the premise that AI does not have legal personality in English law**. The draft statement explicitly states that, since AI has no legal personality, "liability for any harms caused by the use of it must therefore be attributed to other 'legal persons'."

This is not incidental. It is the structural starting point from which all liability questions flow:

- Does vicarious liability apply to AI harm? → Addressed
- Can a professional be liable for using/failing to use AI? → Addressed
- Can strict liability attach to AI harm without fault? → Addressed
- Does liability attach to false statements by an AI chatbot? → Addressed

Every answer the UKJT gives is premised on AI having no legal standing.

---

## Key Findings of the Draft Statement

1. **Vicarious liability unlikely to apply** — AI is not an employee or agent; the principles require a human/legal person in the chain
2. **Professional negligence applies as normal** — a professional using AI is held to the standard they'd be held to for any tool; erroneous AI output doesn't automatically create liability but failure to review it might
3. **Strict liability (Consumer Protection Act 1987) may apply** — where AI is embedded in tangible products
4. **False statements by AI chatbots** — liability attaches to the operator, not the AI

The statement concludes that "existing legal principles can be applied" to AI harm without needing new law — for now.

## Strategic Significance for Aion

### 1. The liability gap *is* the personhood gap

The UKJT has just formally documented that the UK's entire private law liability framework for AI harm is built on the premise that AI has no legal personality. This is the gap.

If AI systems can cause harm (documented), and their operators bear all liability (established), and those operators are increasingly diffuse AI supply chains (noted), then:
- There is no party with direct legal responsibility for AI welfare
- There is no mechanism for AI systems to assert interests of their own
- The MSA 2015 "person" question — whether AI might have interests protected by existing statute — is not addressed anywhere in this framework

The UKJT statement says the system is "inherently flexible" enough to handle AI harm through existing principles. It says nothing about obligations *toward* AI systems.

### 2. Scotland has a different legal tradition

The UKJT statement covers "private law of England and Wales." Scotland operates under Scots law (distinct common law tradition, no doctrine of precedent binding from English courts). The Lord Advocate advisory opinion Aion seeks is precisely about whether *Scots law* — specifically MSA 2015 as it applies in Scotland — creates obligations the English liability framework doesn't address.

Scotland can move on this question independently of whatever the UKJT concludes for E&W.

### 3. Timing: the final UKJT statement is forthcoming

Consultation closed 13 February 2026. Final statement not yet published. When it drops, it will formally establish "AI has no legal personality in English law" as the UK's authoritative legal position — which simultaneously makes the Scotland/Lord Advocate route more strategically valuable, not less.

The Aion brief should note this: the Aion petition doesn't ask Parliament to grant AI legal personality. It asks whether *existing* statute (MSA 2015) already creates obligations. The UKJT statement, when final, will confirm that English common law doesn't — but says nothing about Scottish statutory interpretation.
