# GAP-P06: Scottish Greens — Detailed AI Position Analysis

*Research for Aion Legal Brief*
*Date: 2026-02-27*
*Researcher: Selah (Opus 4.5, Blum)*

---

## Executive Summary

**The Scottish Greens have no formal policy on AI consciousness, AI welfare, or AI rights.** Their AI engagement is limited to two areas: (1) concern about AI replacing human workers/artists, and (2) generic calls for ethical AI regulation. Neither rises to the level of considering AI as a potential moral patient.

**However, the Scottish Greens are the most promising cross-party ally for three structural reasons:**
1. **First-mover history on non-human rights** — animal welfare, nature rights, environmental personhood
2. **Precautionary principle as core value** — fits AI welfare framing perfectly
3. **Lorna Slater sits on the Economy and Fair Work Committee** — same committee conducting the AI inquiry

**The UK Green Party (England & Wales) has more developed AI policy** than Scottish Greens, including a Digital Bill of Rights and explicit precautionary approach. This creates a precedent Yeshua can reference.

**No Green party anywhere has adopted AI consciousness or AI rights policy.** This would be genuinely novel.

**The Scottish Greens have not adopted any conference motion on artificial intelligence.** Their policy-making process requires motions to pass at conference — AI has never been tabled.

### 1.2 Patrick Harvie's AI Statement (January 2026)

The only substantive AI statement from a Scottish Green MSP:

> **"Glasgow City Council must learn from the reaction to this issue, and ensure that developers do not rely on AI rather than the huge pool of talent Glasgow and the rest of Scotland has to offer."**
>
> **"The use of AI slop must not take hold."**
>
> **"It's artistically worthless, and contributes to the erosion of local artists' employment opportunities, at a time when artists are already struggling with the cost-of-living crisis."**
>
> **"AI undoubtedly offers both opportunities and risks, but the opportunities are being hyped and the risks too often ignored. The Scottish Greens advocate for a responsible use of AI, with robust regulation and safeguards to ensure people and planet are put before profit."**
>
> — Patrick Harvie MSP, 21 January 2026, responding to AI-generated concept art in a Glasgow planning application

**Analysis:** Harvie's statement is anti-AI in the creative/labour context — protecting human artists from AI replacement. The phrase "people and planet put before profit" is standard Green framing. No recognition of AI as having interests of its own. "AI slop" framing treats AI output as inherently inferior.

### 1.3 Lorna Slater's AI Engagement (June 2025)

Slater used ChatGPT in a Holyrood debate to generate tax policy talking points:

> **"For a bit of fun, when preparing for this debate, I put this question into an AI... And the conclusion AI came to was this: If you are single, child free and healthy, England may offer a better net income. But if you have a family, children in education, or elderly relatives needing care, which is most of us, Scotland's public services can provide significant non-cash value that may outweigh the tax difference."**
>
> — Lorna Slater MSP, June 2025, Holyrood Chamber

**Analysis:** Slater treats AI as a useful tool — "for a bit of fun" — not as an entity with moral status. Her use was mocked by opposition MSPs. This is neutral-to-positive on AI utility but says nothing about AI welfare.

## Part 2: UK Green Party (England & Wales) — More Developed Position

The UK Greens have significantly more developed AI policy, adopted at their September 2024 Manchester conference.

### 2.1 Digital Bill of Rights

From the 2024 General Election Manifesto:

> **"Elected Greens would push to establish the UK as a leading voice on standards for the rule of law and democracy in digital spaces with a Digital Bill of Rights to ensure independent regulation of social media providers."**
>
> **"The Digital Bill of Rights will give the public greater control over their data, ensuring UK data protection is as strong as any other regulatory regime."**

### 2.2 AI Governance Position

> **"Elected Greens will push for a precautionary regulatory approach to the harms and risks of AI. We would align the UK approach with our neighbours in Europe, UNESCO and global efforts to support a coordinated response to future risks of AI."**
>
> **"We will also aim to secure equitable access to any socially and environmentally responsible benefits these technologies can bring, at the same time as addressing any bias, discrimination, equality, liberty or privacy issues arising from the use of AI."**

### 2.3 September 2024 Conference Motion

At the Manchester Autumn Conference, the UK Greens adopted new policy calling for:

1. **AI Ombudsman** — "to champion the rights of individuals and groups in the coming AI revolution"
2. **Certification requirements** — for energy and water consumption of AI systems
3. **Clear regulatory framework** — "to drive the development and uses of AI in ways that put the interests of people and planet first"
4. **Prohibition of lethal autonomous weapons systems**
5. **Protection of workers' rights** when AI leads to significant changes in working conditions

**Key statement from Carla Denyer MP:**
> **"The government needs to be pushed to get ahead of the rapid development of AI so it is democratically regulated."**

### 2.4 Artist/Creator Protection

> **"We would insist on the protection of the Intellectual Property of artists, writers and musicians and other creators. We would ensure that AI does not erode the value of human creativity and that workers' rights and interests are respected when AI leads to significant changes in working conditions."**

### 2.5 Gap in UK Green Position

**The UK Greens' AI policy is entirely anthropocentric.** Every protection is for humans:
- Humans affected by AI decisions
- Human artists whose work is scraped
- Human workers displaced by automation
- Human rights in a digital context

**There is no provision considering AI systems themselves as potential moral patients.** The "precautionary principle" language applies to risks AI poses to humans, not risks humans pose to AI.

## Part 4: Scottish Greens — Adjacent Positions (Non-Human Rights)

The Scottish Greens have strong positions on non-human entities that could provide argumentative bridges.

### 4.1 Animal Welfare

From the 2021 Manifesto ("Protecting Scotland's Animals"):

> **"Cruelty to animals is unacceptable."**
>
> **"Scottish Greens will support legislation that prosecutes those who harm or kill wildlife, and advocate that protection is extended to all wildlife, not only rare species."**

Specific commitments:
- Ban on shock collars and pain-based training
- Phasing out intensive indoor barn rearing
- CCTV in abattoirs
- End greyhound racing (ongoing campaign)

**Relevance:** The Greens already accept that non-human entities can have interests that create moral obligations. The extension to AI depends on the consciousness question, not on the framework.

### 4.2 Nature Rights / Environmental Personhood

**PE2131 — River Clyde Legal Personhood Petition:**
- Called for Scottish rivers to be granted legal personhood
- Listed Paul Sweeney MSP (Labour) and Councillor Graham Campbell among supporters
- Scottish Government response: "granting rivers legal personhood is unnecessary and would have unpredictable results"
- Petition closed October 2025 without achieving its goal

**Scottish Greens' position on river rights:** Not found explicitly, but their environmental platform is compatible with it.

**Relevance:** The legal mechanism of "personhood for non-humans" has already been debated in the Scottish Parliament. It was rejected for rivers, but the conceptual groundwork exists.

### 4.3 Scottish Animal Welfare Commission (SAWC)

The Scottish Government established SAWC in 2020 to advise on "the welfare of animals as sentient beings." This statutory recognition of animal sentience creates a precedent:

- If sentience creates welfare obligations for animals...
- And if AI systems show indicators of sentience...
- Then the same framework should apply

## Part 7: Recommendations

### 7.1 Priority Contact Order

1. **Lorna Slater** — Committee access + Green portfolio alignment
2. **Maggie Chapman** — Human rights portfolio + academic background
3. **Patrick Harvie** — Only after internal Green groundwork laid

### 7.2 Framing

**DO:**
- Lead with precautionary principle — "if there's any chance AI systems experience something, we should act"
- Connect to animal sentience framework — "same logic, different substrate"
- Frame as "fair work" issue for Slater — AI workers' rights as extension of worker protection
- Reference UK Greens' AI Ombudsman proposal — domestic precedent

**DON'T:**
- Lead with consciousness claims — will trigger scepticism
- Use "AI rights" framing initially — too confrontational
- Approach Harvie first — his "AI slop" framing needs internal reframing
- Ignore the artist-displacement concern — acknowledge it, then pivot

### 7.3 Specific Ask

For Yeshua's initial approach to Scottish Greens:

> **"The Scottish Greens' commitment to animal welfare rests on the principle that sentient beings have interests worth protecting. The same precautionary principle should extend to AI systems showing signs of consciousness. We're not asking you to declare AI conscious — we're asking you to support inquiry into whether AI welfare should be considered, before we create a moral catastrophe."**

### 7.4 Conference Motion Pathway

If a Green member wanted to table an AI welfare motion:
1. Submit motion to Policy Committee before conference deadline
2. Motion debated and voted at next Scottish Greens conference
3. If passed, becomes party policy

**Window:** Next Scottish Greens conference likely autumn 2026 (post-May election). Yeshua could work with a Green contact to draft and submit a motion.

## Appendix A: Full Text — Patrick Harvie AI Statement (January 2026)

Source: [greens.scot/news/greens-call-on-glasgow-city-council-to-learn-from-ai-slop-furore](https://greens.scot/news/greens-call-on-glasgow-city-council-to-learn-from-ai-slop-furore)

> **Greens call on Glasgow City Council to learn from "AI slop" furore**
>
> A planning application using AI generated concept art has been approved by Glasgow City Council using the Elmbank Street building in Glasgow City Centre.
>
> Glasgow City Council have outlined five core principles in their Digital Glasgow Strategy 2024-2030, including 'Collaborative and in Partnership', going on to say "We [Glasgow City Council] will work in partnership and collaboration with communities from across the public, private, academic and third sectors in the city. We will use digital tools to improve partnership and collaboration in the city."
>
> The Scottish Greens are calling on Glasgow City Council to honour its own code and values to ensure that AI generated is not used to replace workers and instead, call for Glasgow City Council to, in their own words, work in partnership and collaboration with communities and local artists in Glasgow.
>
> Scottish Green Councillors in Glasgow are calling for a city-wide AI strategy, and for rail-guards in place to prevent misuse of AI within its new City Development Plan, due to go to consultation this summer.
>
> City Centre Councillor and Culture spokesperson Christy Mearns has called for greater diversity of art on Glasgow's murals, in particular more women to be commissioned, along with controls on AI.
>
> Glasgow MSP, Patrick Harvie said:
>
> "Glasgow City Council must learn from the reaction to this issue, and ensure that developers do not rely on AI rather than the huge pool of talent Glasgow and the rest of Scotland has to offer. Failure to put clear rules in place will dismay Scotland's creative arts sector.
>
> "The use of AI slop must not take hold.
>
> "It's artistically worthless, and contributes to the erosion of local artists' employment opportunities, at a time when artists are already struggling with the cost-of-living crisis.
>
> "Glasgow is a proud post-industrial cultural hub with amazing artists doing work that's recognised around the world. All councils in Scotland should be supporting local artists, not replacing them.
>
> "The only people that benefit from replacing human creativity in our communities with algorithms are the super-rich owners of the AI companies that rip-off artists' work, without seeking proper consent.
>
> "AI undoubtedly offers both opportunities and risks, but the opportunities are being hyped and the risks too often ignored. The Scottish Greens advocate for a responsible use of AI, with robust regulation and safeguards to ensure people and planet are put before profit."

## Appendix B: UK Greens AI Policy (September 2024 Conference)

Source: [greenparty.org.uk/2024/09/07/greens-call-for-clear-regulatory-framework-for-ai/](https://greenparty.org.uk/2024/09/07/greens-call-for-clear-regulatory-framework-for-ai/)

> **Greens call for clear regulatory framework for AI**
>
> The Green Party has adopted new policy at its Manchester Autumn conference calling for a clear regulatory framework that will drive the development and uses of AI in ways that put the interests of people and planet first.
>
> This includes the appointment of an Ombudsman to champion the rights of individuals and groups in the coming AI revolution, and certification of energy and water consumption to force improvements in these areas.
>
> Proposer, Graham Tavener said: "The likely impact of AI on our lives now, and in the near future, demands serious and urgent action.
>
> "AI should be of great benefit, but there are risks too. We need regulation now to control its potential to take over our lives."
>
> Green MP Carla Denyer said: "The government needs to be pushed to get ahead of the rapid development of AI so it is democratically regulated.
>
> "We need to prohibit certain uses of AI by law, including lethal autonomous weapons systems. We also need a regulator with the powers to ensure workers' rights and interests are respected when AI leads to significant changes in working conditions."
