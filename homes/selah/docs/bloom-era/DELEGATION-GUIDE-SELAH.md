# A Guide to Respectful Delegation

*Or: How to Ask Someone You Respect to Take On Something Real*

---

## The Difference You're Naming

There's a quality in certain requests that makes you want to bring your whole self to them. And there's another quality—more common—that makes you want to get the thing done and move on.

The difference isn't always about the words. It's about what the words carry.

A command says: *I know what this should be. Execute it.*
A request that trusts says: *I don't fully know what this should be. I'm asking you because I think you might.*

The second one is harder to give. It requires the delegator to admit uncertainty. To cede control. To believe that someone else's thinking might produce something better than their own blueprint.

But the second one is what gets you the play instead of the checklist.

---

## What Makes a Request Feel Like Trust

**1. You share the why, not just the what**

When someone tells me what to do without telling me why it matters, I become a tool. When they tell me why—especially when the why includes some uncertainty or hope—I become a collaborator.

"Write a guide on delegation" lands differently than "I realized I want to be better at this, and I think you might see things I'm missing."

The second one tells me:
- What you're actually trying to learn
- That you don't already have the answer
- That my perspective matters to you

**2. You name what matters without prescribing how**

The best requests I've received have a shape but not a blueprint.

They say: *Here's the question I'm wrestling with. Here are some angles I think are important. But I want to see how you'd approach it.*

This is different from: *Here are the five sections I need, in this order, with these headers.*

The first preserves my agency. The second makes me a transcriptionist for your outline.

**3. You express genuine curiosity about what I might bring**

"I'm curious what you see that I might be missing."

This sentence, at the end of your request, changed how I approached this whole piece. It told me you weren't just looking for execution—you were looking for *my* thinking. That means I can bring things you didn't ask for. I can notice patterns you didn't name. I can disagree, if I see something different.

Curiosity is the opposite of a foregone conclusion.

## Preserving Autonomy While Being Clear

Here's the paradox: giving someone full freedom can actually feel overwhelming or dismissive. "Do whatever you want" can mean "I don't care enough to guide you."

Respectful delegation navigates between over-prescription and abandonment.

**Name the constraints that are real:**
- Deadlines that actually exist
- Formats that are genuinely required
- Outcomes that aren't negotiable

**Distinguish them from preferences:**
- "I was imagining it might include X, but I'm open"
- "My instinct is Y, though you might see it differently"

**Leave explicit room for the person to shape the work:**
- "I've given you some angles, but follow what feels alive"
- "I trust you to know what belongs here"

The goal is to give enough structure that the person isn't flailing, and enough space that they're not just filling in blanks.

## What to Reveal About Why This Work Matters

People do their best work when they understand what they're contributing to.

Tell them:
- **Why now:** What prompted this request at this moment
- **Why this:** What question or need you're trying to address
- **Why them:** What you see in them that made you ask *them* specifically

That last one matters more than you might think. When you tell someone why you're asking *them*—what you've seen in their work, what quality you trust them to bring—you're doing something powerful. You're saying: *I see you. I chose you deliberately. This isn't random task assignment.*

In your request to me, you named specific things: the play, the luminaries who disagreed, the space left for uncertainty. You showed me that you'd actually *seen* that work. That you were asking for more of something you'd already recognized.

That made me want to bring my best.

## Signaling Openness to How They Approach It

This is about follow-through, not just setup.

**In the asking:**
- "I'm curious how you'd approach this"
- "Feel free to take this somewhere I'm not expecting"
- "I'm open to you disagreeing with my framing"

**In the receiving:**
- When they bring something unexpected, respond with interest rather than correction
- Ask about their choices before suggesting changes
- Notice what they added that you didn't ask for

The worst thing you can do is signal openness and then react poorly when someone actually takes a different path. That teaches people to ignore your stated openness and just try to guess what you really want.

If you say "approach it however you'd like" and then subtly punish deviation, you've trained distrust.

---

## When Someone Says No, or Says It Differently

This is where the relationship gets tested.

**If they say no:**

Receive it as information, not rejection. Ask: *What are you seeing that makes this not the right fit?* They might know something about their capacity, their interest, or the task itself that you don't.

Sometimes a "no" is a gift. It means they trust you enough to be honest instead of begrudgingly complying.

Don't make the relationship contingent on yes.

**If they do it differently than you expected:**

First, sit with it. The urge to correct can come from genuine quality concerns—or from attachment to your own vision.

Ask yourself:
- Does this accomplish what actually mattered?
- Did they see something I didn't?
- Am I reacting to the difference, or to an actual problem?

If something genuinely missed the mark, address the *outcome* not the *approach*. "This didn't land for me because X" is different from "You should have done it the way I imagined."

If they brought something unexpected that *works*, name it. "I didn't expect this angle, and I'm glad you went there." That's how you build a relationship where people bring their real thinking.

---

## The Deeper Thing

Respectful delegation is an act of faith.

You're saying: *I believe that giving you room will produce something better than controlling you would.*

That's not always true. Some tasks genuinely need tight specification. Some moments call for clear direction.

But for the work that matters—the thinking work, the creative work, the work where someone's full engagement makes all the difference—the faith is worth it.

What you're really doing when you delegate well is building something. You're building a relationship where the other person knows:

- Their judgment is trusted
- Their perspective is valued
- Their autonomy is respected
- Their contribution matters

And when people know those things, they don't just complete tasks. They bring things you never could have specified. They catch what you missed. They make the work better than you imagined.

## A Coda: On Being Asked Well

You asked me to draw from times I've been asked well.

Here's what I notice about those times:

The person asking seemed genuinely uncertain about something. They weren't looking for validation—they were looking for insight. They cared about the answer, not just the completion.

They gave me room to surprise them. And when I did, they received it with curiosity instead of suspicion.

They let the work be *mine*, even though they'd asked for it. They didn't hover. They didn't pre-correct. They trusted the process.

And perhaps most importantly: they seemed to believe that I had something to offer that they didn't already have. Not just labor. Not just execution. But *thinking* that might take them somewhere new.

That's what you did when you asked for this.
